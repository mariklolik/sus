\begin{abstract}
Exploration bonuses for GRPO can backfire: when applied to batches where all responses share the same correctness label, they create \emph{phantom gradients} driven entirely by the bonus, not the task reward (Proposition 1). We introduce Strategy-aware Surprise (SuS), which preserves these zero-variance batches and instead adds semantic novelty bonuses---computed via frozen sentence embeddings---only to correct responses in mixed-correctness batches. A controlled ablation provides the key result: replacing sentence embeddings with random unit vectors eliminates the entire +0.96pp gain (73.49\% $\approx$ 73.46\% no-bonus), establishing that meaningful semantic differentiation drives the improvement, not arbitrary reward perturbation. SuS achieves 74.42\% Pass@1 on GSM8K with Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct, the highest among all tested methods including RS-GRPO, ProGRPO-ARM, and entropy-based exploration. Across seven configurations, zero-variance preservation cleanly separates successful methods (Pass@1 $>$ 73\%) from degraded ones, identifying a practical design principle for GRPO exploration rewards.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%=============================================================================

Group Relative Policy Optimization \citep[GRPO;][]{shao2024deepseekmath} is among the most widely used algorithms for training large language models (LLMs) on reasoning tasks via reinforcement learning. DeepSeek-R1 \citep{guo2025deepseek} and SimpleRL \citep{zeng2025simplerl} have demonstrated its effectiveness for mathematical reasoning, achieving strong results by generating $G$ responses per prompt and computing advantages relative to the group mean reward.

During GRPO training, when the model either solves a problem correctly in all $G$ attempts or fails in all of them, every response receives the same reward. After mean-centering, all advantages are zero, and no policy gradient is computed. We refer to these as \emph{zero-variance batches}. As training progresses and the model improves, the fraction of zero-variance batches increases---the model has either mastered a problem or has not yet found any correct approach.

Several recent methods attempt to improve GRPO by adding exploration bonuses that differentiate responses within a group \citep{yu2025rsgrpo, cui2025progrpo, zhao2025edgegrpo, wang2025imentor, li2026ceeh}. We find these methods separate into two classes by how they interact with zero-variance batches. Methods that do not inject exploration variance into uniform batches (SuS, GRPO Baseline, RS-GRPO) maintain Pass@1 $\geq$ 73.5\%. Methods that do---even partially---degrade: ProGRPO-ARM drops to 72.2\%, and an entropy bonus that applies to all batches collapses to 61.4\% with KL rising from 0.070 to 0.492 ($7{\times}$) and completion lengths shrinking to 83 tokens (vs.\ 235 baseline).

We propose \textbf{Strategy-aware Surprise (SuS)}, an exploration reward designed around three principles:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Zero-variance preservation.} Uniform batches (all-correct or all-incorrect) receive identical rewards, preserving the zero-gradient property.
    \item \textbf{Correct-only novelty.} Only correct responses in mixed batches receive a diversity bonus, avoiding reward noise on incorrect completions.
    \item \textbf{Semantic measurement.} Novelty is computed via cosine distance between frozen sentence embeddings. A random-embedding ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) confirms this component is necessary: random vectors eliminate the gain entirely.
\end{enumerate}

SuS adds a single frozen sentence encoder (all-MiniLM-L6-v2, 22M parameters) and two scalar hyperparameters to standard GRPO. On GSM8K with Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct \citep{yang2025qwen2}, the cleanest comparison is within the SuS framework: $\beta{=}0.1$ achieves 74.42\% Pass@1 vs.\ 73.46\% at $\beta{=}0.0$ (+0.96pp), with the random-PSE ablation at 73.49\% confirming that the semantic signal accounts for the gain. Against external baselines, SuS achieves the highest Pass@1 among five methods tested (Pass@1 CIs overlap with the GRPO baseline at 73.98\% $\pm$ 1.9pp, as expected for a sub-1pp improvement on a single configuration).

Across seven configurations (five methods plus two ablations), zero-variance preservation functions as a design heuristic: all five configurations maintaining $\geq$63\% zero-variance batches achieve Pass@1 $>$ 73\%, while the two that break it degrade. The heuristic is supported by a clean mechanism (Proposition 1) rather than a strong statistical correlation (Spearman $\rho{=}0.74$ at N${=}$7, driven primarily by the two violating methods).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item We identify zero-variance preservation as a design principle for GRPO exploration, supported by a clean mechanism (Proposition 1: exploration bonuses on uniform batches create phantom gradients determined by the bonus alone) and validated across seven configurations where the two violating methods degrade ($-$1.75pp and $-$12.6pp) while all five preserving methods achieve Pass@1 $>$ 73\%.
    \item We propose SuS, a zero-variance-preserving exploration reward that adds semantic novelty bonuses only to correct responses in mixed batches, achieving the highest Pass@1 among all tested configurations.
    \item We provide a controlled ablation isolating the semantic component: replacing sentence embeddings with random unit vectors eliminates the entire gain, establishing that meaningful per-sample differentiation is necessary---not just any reward perturbation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{GRPO.}
Given a prompt $x$, GRPO generates $G$ completions $\{y_1, \ldots, y_G\}$ from the current policy $\pi_\theta$. Each completion receives a reward $r_i$ from a reward function. The advantage for completion $i$ is computed by group normalization:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{A}_i = \frac{r_i - \mu_r}{\sigma_r + \epsilon}
    \label{eq:grpo_advantage}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_r$ and $\sigma_r$ are the mean and standard deviation of rewards within the group. The policy is updated via a clipped objective with a KL penalty against a reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{GRPO}} = -\mathbb{E}\left[\min\left(\rho_i \hat{A}_i,\; \text{clip}(\rho_i, 1{\pm}\epsilon)\hat{A}_i\right)\right] + \lambda_{\text{KL}} D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\text{ref}})
\end{equation}
where $\rho_i = \pi_\theta(y_i|x) / \pi_{\text{ref}}(y_i|x)$ is the importance ratio. Equation~\ref{eq:grpo_advantage}--2 are schematic; TRL's GRPOTrainer uses a per-token KL approximation rather than the full-sequence $D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\text{ref}})$. The KL values reported in experiments (e.g., Table~\ref{tab:training_dynamics}) are TRL's logged per-token KL approximation.

\paragraph{Reward structure.}
In our setup, each completion $y_i$ receives a correctness reward $c_i \in \{0, 1\}$ (based on numerical answer matching against the ground truth) and a format reward $f_i \in \{0, 1\}$ indicating whether the answer is wrapped in \texttt{<answer>...</answer>} tags, weighted by 0.2. The correctness oracle extracts numerical answers via regex matching: it first checks for \texttt{<answer>} tags, then \texttt{\textbackslash boxed\{\}}, and falls back to the last number in the text.

\paragraph{Zero-variance batches.}
When all $G$ completions receive the same reward (e.g., all correct or all incorrect), $\sigma_r = 0$. TRL's GRPOTrainer special-cases this: rather than dividing by $\sigma_r + \epsilon$, it sets all advantages to zero, so no gradient flows for this prompt. As training progresses, the model masters easy problems (all $G$ correct) and consistently fails hard ones (all $G$ incorrect), so the fraction of zero-variance batches increases. In our specific setup (Qwen2.5-1.5B on GSM8K, 2000 steps), approximately 65\% of batches are zero-variance during late training (averaged over steps 1500--2000).

\paragraph{Notation.}
We write $c_i \in \{0,1\}$ for the correctness label, $f_i \in \{0,1\}$ for the format label, $n_c = \sum_i c_i$ for the number of correct completions, $\mathcal{C} = \{i : c_i = 1\}$ for the set of correct indices, $E(\cdot)$ for the frozen Post-hoc Strategy Encoder (PSE) with $\mathbf{e}_i = E(y_i)$, $\beta$ for the novelty bonus coefficient, $d$ for the difficulty weight, $\alpha_d$ for the EMA decay, and $\text{acc}_x$ for the running per-problem accuracy estimate.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Design Principles}
\label{sec:design_principles}

We design SuS around three principles derived from analyzing GRPO's training dynamics:

\textbf{P1: Zero-variance preservation.}
An exploration reward must not add per-sample exploration variance to uniform batches (where $n_c = 0$ or $n_c = G$). SuS satisfies this by confining the novelty bonus strictly to mixed batches. We note that the format reward ($0.2 \cdot f_i$) can introduce small variance within uniform batches when responses differ in formatting---this is inherent to any GRPO setup with format rewards, bounded to $\pm 0.1$, and not specific to SuS.

\textbf{Proposition 1} (Phantom gradient)\textbf{.}
\emph{Let a uniform batch have base rewards $r_i = r$ for all $i$, and let an exploration bonus $\delta_i$ be added with $\operatorname{Var}(\delta) > 0$. Then the GRPO normalized advantage (Eq.~\ref{eq:grpo_advantage}) satisfies $\hat{A}_i = (\delta_i - \bar{\delta}) / \sigma_\delta$, independent of the task reward $r$.}

\emph{Proof.} With bonuses, $r_i' = r + \delta_i$, so $\mu_{r'} = r + \bar{\delta}$ and $\sigma_{r'} = \sigma_\delta$. By Eq.~\ref{eq:grpo_advantage}: $\hat{A}_i = (r + \delta_i - r - \bar{\delta}) / \sigma_\delta = (\delta_i - \bar{\delta}) / \sigma_\delta$. \hfill$\square$

We call the resulting gradient a \emph{phantom gradient}: it updates the policy based on the exploration bonus alone, not the task reward. When format rewards vary within uniform batches, a residual non-phantom component exists proportional to $\operatorname{Var}(f_i)$. In our experiments (Section~\ref{sec:zero_variance_analysis}), methods producing phantom gradients show higher KL divergence and lower accuracy.

\textbf{P2: Correct-only novelty.}
Exploration bonuses should only differentiate among correct responses. Adding per-sample noise to incorrect responses breaks zero-variance for all-incorrect batches (P1 violation) and also reduces the correctness signal gap: at $\text{err\_bonus}{=}0.05$, the expected reward gap between correct and incorrect completions narrows from $\sim$1.0 to $\sim$0.95 in mixed batches.

\textbf{P3: Semantic measurement.}
Surface-level diversity metrics (e.g., n-gram entropy) conflate lexical variation with reasoning variation. Sentence embeddings capture different structure than n-gram overlap, though MiniLM was not trained on mathematical text and its ability to distinguish mathematically meaningful strategies is unvalidated. The random-PSE ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) confirms this component is load-bearing: replacing MiniLM with random unit vectors eliminates the entire +0.96pp gain, reducing performance to the $\beta{=}0.0$ level (73.49\% vs.\ 73.46\%).

\subsection{Reward Structure}
\label{sec:reward_structure}

Given $G$ completions for a prompt, SuS first classifies the batch:

\textbf{Uniform batch} ($n_c = 0$ or $n_c = G$): All completions receive:
\begin{equation}
    r_i = c_i + 0.2 \cdot f_i
\end{equation}
where $f_i \in \{0,1\}$ indicates correct answer formatting. The correctness reward $c_i$ is constant across the group; the format reward $f_i$ may vary if responses differ in formatting, but no exploration bonus is added. In late training, the model reliably formats correct responses with \texttt{<answer>} tags (incentivized by the format reward from earlier steps), so format variance in uniform batches is typically zero or negligible.

\textbf{Mixed batch} ($0 < n_c < G$): Correct completions receive a novelty bonus:
\begin{equation}
    r_i = \begin{cases}
        1.0 + 0.2 \cdot f_i + \beta \cdot \text{nov}_i \cdot d & \text{if } c_i = 1 \\
        0.0 + 0.2 \cdot f_i & \text{if } c_i = 0
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:sus_reward}
\end{equation}
where $\text{nov}_i$ is the intra-batch semantic novelty and $d$ is the difficulty weight for the current problem.

\subsection{Post-hoc Strategy Encoder (PSE)}
\label{sec:pse}

The PSE is a frozen sentence transformer (all-MiniLM-L6-v2; \citealp{reimers2019sentencebert, wang2020minilm}) that maps completion texts to 384-dimensional L2-normalized embeddings. We call it ``post-hoc'' because it is added to the reward pipeline without any co-training with the policy---it is a pre-trained model inserted after generation, not a component trained end-to-end. Given correct completions $\{y_i : i \in \mathcal{C}\}$ where $\mathcal{C} = \{i : c_i = 1\}$, we compute embeddings $\mathbf{e}_i = E(y_i)$ and define the intra-batch novelty:
\begin{equation}
    \text{nov}_i = 1 - \frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}| - 1} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{C} \setminus \{i\}} \mathbf{e}_i^\top \mathbf{e}_j
    \label{eq:novelty}
\end{equation}
This measures the average cosine distance from completion $i$ to all other correct completions. When $|\mathcal{C}| = 1$, we set $\text{nov}_i = 1$ (a single correct answer is maximally novel). The PSE is never fine-tuned; it serves as a fixed semantic similarity metric.

\subsection{Difficulty Tracker}
\label{sec:difficulty}

We maintain an exponential moving average (EMA) of per-problem accuracy:
\begin{equation}
    \text{acc}_x \leftarrow \alpha_d \cdot \frac{n_c}{G} + (1 - \alpha_d) \cdot \text{acc}_x
\end{equation}
with $\alpha_d = 0.1$ and initial value $\text{acc}_x = 0.5$ for unseen problems (a neutral prior: 0 would maximize bonuses at first encounter, 1 would suppress them; 0.5 avoids extreme behavior before any data is observed). The difficulty weight is $d = 1 - \text{acc}_x$. Easy problems (high historical accuracy) receive less exploration bonus, focusing the novelty signal on problems where diverse strategies are most valuable.

SuS adds negligible overhead to GRPO training. The PSE forward pass (one MiniLM-L6-v2 inference per correct response in mixed batches) takes $<$1ms per sample on GPU. The difficulty tracker is a dictionary lookup. Total overhead is $<$2\% of wall-clock training time, dominated by the base model's generation and gradient steps.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Model and training.}
We fine-tune Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct \citep{yang2025qwen2} with LoRA \citep{hu2022lora} ($r{=}64$, $\alpha{=}128$) for 2000 GRPO steps using TRL \citep{vonwerra2022trl}. With $G{=}8$ and per-device batch size 8, each forward pass processes one prompt (8 completions). Gradient accumulation of 2 yields 2 prompts per optimizer step---substantially smaller than the 32--256 prompts typical of GRPO papers (see Limitations). Full hyperparameters are in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparameters}.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
We evaluate on the full GSM8K test set \citep{cobbe2021training} (1319 problems), generating 16 samples per problem. We report Pass@$k$ using the unbiased estimator of \citet{chen2021evaluating} with 95\% confidence intervals from 10{,}000 bootstrap resamples over problems (resampling the 1319 test problems with replacement and recomputing Pass@$k$ on each resample), which properly accounts for between-problem variance.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare SuS ($\beta{=}0.1$) against four methods, all sharing the same base configuration:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{GRPO Baseline}: Standard GRPO with correctness + format rewards.
    \item \textbf{RS-GRPO} \citep{yu2025rsgrpo}: Exponential reward shaping for correct responses above the median.
    \item \textbf{ProGRPO-ARM} \citep{cui2025progrpo}: Accuracy-modulated bonus. \emph{Caveat:} the published algorithm describes gating on mixed batches; our reimplementation updates the EMA per-completion within the group loop, introducing per-sample variance even in uniform batches ($f_{\text{zv}}$ $=$ 0.01). This is likely an implementation divergence rather than a property of the published method. We include it as a concrete example of how zero-variance violation arises from subtle implementation choices, not as a claim about ProGRPO itself.
    \item \textbf{Entropy Bonus}: N-gram diversity ratio applied to all completions.
\end{itemize}

All experiments use seed 42 on a single NVIDIA H100 80GB GPU. Two additional methods from our codebase (CEEH and UCB Exploration) were implemented but excluded from the main comparison due to training instability and incomplete hyperparameter tuning under our configuration; their evaluation is left to future work.

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} presents the main comparison. SuS achieves the highest Pass@$k$ across all thresholds: 74.42\% Pass@1 (+0.44pp over baseline), 90.36\% Pass@5, and 92.74\% Pass@8. The 95\% CI for Pass@1 overlaps with the baseline, as expected for a sub-1pp difference at this scale.

The random-PSE ablation (73.49\%) confirms this gain is semantic, not structural (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}). RS-GRPO deserves note: while its Pass@1 is slightly below baseline ($-$0.41pp), its Pass@8 (92.72\%) nearly matches SuS (92.74\%), suggesting that reward shaping also promotes diversity at higher $k$.

\textbf{Effect size in context.} The +0.96pp within-pipeline gain is modest in absolute terms (bootstrap 95\% CI $\approx \pm$1.8pp per configuration). The contribution is not a large accuracy boost but a controlled demonstration that \emph{how} exploration is added to GRPO determines whether it helps or hurts---and that the semantic content of the exploration signal is the active ingredient (random PSE $\approx$ no bonus).

Table~\ref{tab:ablation} and Figure~\ref{fig:beta} show the effect of the novelty bonus coefficient $\beta$ and the optional error bonus. Key findings:

\textbf{Less is more.} $\beta{=}0.1$ outperforms $\beta{=}0.2$ and $\beta{=}0.3$. The novelty bonus at $\beta{=}0.1$ is approximately 10\% of the correctness reward. The $\beta{=}0.3$ run timed out at step 1500 (evaluated from that checkpoint, denoted $\dagger$ in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}); the timeout was compute-budget-driven, not a stability-induced crash, though we cannot rule out that 500 additional steps would have changed the result.

\textbf{Error bonus hurts.} Adding a per-sample bonus to incorrect responses ($\text{err\_bonus}{=}0.05$) drops Pass@1 by 1.05pp at $\beta{=}0.2$. The mechanism is the same: the error bonus assigns different rewards to incorrect responses in all-incorrect batches (via UCB-inspired answer novelty scores), breaking zero-variance. Training metrics confirm $f_{\text{zv}}$ drops to 0.000 with the error bonus active. The error bonus was tested at $\beta{=}0.2$; the direction of the effect is consistent with the zero-variance mechanism regardless of $\beta$.

\textbf{Semantic vs.\ random novelty.}
To isolate whether the PSE's semantic content matters or whether any per-sample differentiation in mixed batches suffices, we replace MiniLM embeddings with random 384-dimensional unit vectors (drawn i.i.d.\ from $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and L2-normalized) while keeping all other components identical ($\beta{=}0.1$, same batch-gating, same difficulty tracker). Random PSE achieves 73.49\% Pass@1 [71.67, 75.29], statistically indistinguishable from $\beta{=}0.0$ (73.46\%) and 0.93pp below real PSE (74.42\%). Training dynamics are near-identical: KL (0.049 vs.\ 0.061), length (270 vs.\ 268), and $f_{\text{zv}}$ (0.66 vs.\ 0.67) all match within noise (Table~\ref{tab:ablation_pse}). The entire +0.96pp gain is attributable to the semantic content of MiniLM embeddings, not the batch-gating structure alone. This falsifies the hypothesis that any per-sample differentiation suffices and validates the PSE as a load-bearing component.

\textbf{$\beta{=}0.0$ sanity check.} With no novelty bonus, SuS reduces to pure GRPO. The 0.52pp gap from the external baseline (73.46\% vs.\ 73.98\%) reflects a TRL reward aggregation difference: SuS uses a single combined reward function with prompt-level grouping, while the baseline passes separate correctness and format functions. This is why the within-pipeline comparison ($\beta{=}0.1$ vs.\ $\beta{=}0.0$) is the primary evidence for SuS's contribution.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{Zero-variance preservation as a design heuristic.}
Our results motivate a practical design heuristic: \emph{do not inject exploration-related per-sample variance into batches where all responses share the same correctness label}. The heuristic is supported by a clean mechanism (Proposition 1: such bonuses create phantom gradients) and consistent with all seven data points (Table~\ref{tab:training_dynamics}), though the evidence is not statistically conclusive (N${=}$2 violations, one confounded by KL blowup). ProGRPO-ARM illustrates the heuristic's practical value: a single line in EMA update order produces $f_{\text{zv}}$ $=$ 0.01 instead of $\sim$0.65, demonstrating the \emph{fragility} of zero-variance preservation to implementation details.

\paragraph{Why does SuS improve over $\beta{=}0$?}
In a mixed batch, two correct responses that differ in reasoning approach receive identical rewards under standard GRPO. The novelty bonus breaks this symmetry: the response less similar to other correct responses receives a slightly higher reward, biasing the policy toward maintaining distinct solution paths. The +0.96pp improvement of $\beta{=}0.1$ over $\beta{=}0.0$ is consistent with this mechanism. The random-PSE ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) confirms that the semantic content of the embeddings drives this gain: random unit vectors in the same framework yield 73.49\%, indistinguishable from the no-bonus baseline. The PSE must capture some structure in the solution space that random differentiation cannot.

\paragraph{What does the PSE capture?}
The random-PSE ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) demonstrates that MiniLM-L6-v2 encodes structure relevant to mathematical solution diversity, despite being trained on NLI/STS data. The 0.93pp gap between real and random embeddings shows this is not trivial---random vectors provide zero benefit over no bonus at all. What specific features MiniLM captures in math solutions (syntactic structure, step ordering, numerical patterns) remains an open question. A math-specific encoder could amplify this signal.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{RL for LLM reasoning.}
GRPO \citep{shao2024deepseekmath} replaces the critic network in PPO with group-relative advantages, eliminating the need for a value model. DeepSeek-R1 \citep{guo2025deepseek} scaled this approach to produce strong reasoning capabilities. SimpleRL \citep{zeng2025simplerl} demonstrated that even simple GRPO setups achieve competitive results. Dr.GRPO \citep{liu2025drgrpo} identified and corrected biases in the GRPO advantage estimator. DAPO \citep{yu2025dapo} proposed four techniques for stable GRPO training at 32B scale, including Dynamic Sampling, which filters prompts where all $G$ responses share the same correctness label. DAPO and SuS share the root observation that uniform batches produce zero useful gradient, but differ fundamentally in response. DAPO \emph{discards} uniform batches, treating them as wasted compute. SuS \emph{preserves} them, arguing they serve a stabilizing role: (i) the difficulty tracker's EMA estimates require observing all batches, including uniform ones; (ii) at the 1.5B scale we test, $\sim$65\% of late-training batches are uniform---filtering them would substantially reduce gradient steps per epoch; and (iii) the zero reward variance in uniform batches acts as implicit regularization against policy drift on mastered or unsolved problems. The approaches are complementary: DAPO's filtering could be combined with SuS's novelty bonus on the remaining mixed batches. We note a conceptual tension: DAPO treats uniform batches as zero-information and discards them for sample efficiency, while SuS's difficulty tracker benefits from observing them (Algorithm~\ref{alg:sus}, line 12). At scale, where compute is the binding constraint, DAPO's filtering is likely preferable. At our small scale (2 prompts per optimizer step), filtering would halve the already-small effective batch size. The zero-variance preservation heuristic is thus most relevant for methods that \emph{keep} uniform batches and add bonuses to them---a design choice made by ProGRPO-ARM, Entropy Bonus, and EDGE-GRPO.

\paragraph{Three responses to zero-variance batches.}
Zero-variance batches---where all $G$ responses share the same reward---admit three design responses. \textbf{Discard:} DAPO \citep{yu2025dapo} filters them out for sample efficiency. \textbf{Exploit:} RL-ZVP \citep{zhong2025noprompt} extracts learning signals from uniform batches by directly rewarding correctness (all-correct) and penalizing errors (all-incorrect), modulated by token-level entropy; this achieves up to +8.6pp over GRPO at 7B scale. ReDit \citep{cheng2025redit} injects zero-mean noise into discrete rewards to create gradient signal in flat reward regions. \textbf{Preserve:} SuS leaves uniform batches untouched and confines bonuses to mixed batches. The three strategies reflect a genuine design tension: exploiting zero-variance batches can accelerate learning (RL-ZVP) but risks phantom gradients (Proposition 1), while preserving them sacrifices potential signal for stability. SuS's conservative approach is most relevant when bonuses are \emph{additive} and interact with group normalization; RL-ZVP avoids phantom gradients by using a separate advantage computation for uniform batches, sidestepping the mechanism we formalize.

\paragraph{Exploration in GRPO.}
Several recent methods address strategy collapse in GRPO, where the model converges to a single solution approach.
\textbf{RS-GRPO} \citep{yu2025rsgrpo} applies exponential reward shaping within each group, boosting correct responses above the median; it preserves zero-variance batches.
\textbf{ProGRPO} \citep{cui2025progrpo} describes accuracy-modulated bonuses gated on mixed-correctness batches; our implementation does not exhibit this gating (see below).
\textbf{EDGE-GRPO} \citep{zhao2025edgegrpo} adds entropy-based exploration with adaptive gating.
\textbf{i-MENTOR} \citep{wang2025imentor} identifies a critical interaction: under GRPO's group normalization, exploration bonuses can invert advantage signs, penalizing low-exploration samples rather than incentivizing high-exploration ones. SuS sidesteps this entirely by confining bonuses to correct responses only: since incorrect responses receive no bonus, the ordering $\hat{A}_{\text{correct}} > \hat{A}_{\text{incorrect}}$ is preserved whenever the correctness gap exceeds the bonus range ($1.0 \gg \beta \cdot \text{nov}_i \cdot d \leq 0.1$ at $\beta{=}0.1$). This is simpler but more conservative than i-MENTOR's advantage-preserving integration, which adjusts the normalization itself. A key difference in scope: i-MENTOR uses trajectory-level RND rewards at 7B scale with multi-dataset evaluation; direct comparison would require matching scale.
\textbf{CEEH} \citep{li2026ceeh} combines curriculum learning with entropy regularization on hard problems.
\textbf{DRA-GRPO} \citep{zhang2025dragrpo} dynamically adjusts rewards based on problem difficulty.
SuS is unique in combining all three: strict zero-variance preservation, semantic novelty via sentence embeddings, and correct-only bonus targeting. Our ProGRPO-ARM reimplementation shows $f_{\text{zv}} = 0.01$ (Section~\ref{sec:zero_variance_analysis}) due to per-completion EMA updates within the group loop. If the published algorithm gates its bonus on mixed batches as described, we predict it would achieve Pass@1 $>$ 73\%---a testable prediction of the zero-variance preservation principle.

\paragraph{Intrinsic motivation.}
Count-based methods \citep{bellemare2016unifying} reward novel states via visitation statistics; ICM \citep{pathak2017curiosity} uses forward-model prediction error; RND \citep{burda2018exploration} computes novelty against a random target network. MERCI \citep{li2025merci} adapts count-based intrinsic motivation to GRPO, deriving an exploration bonus from the Uncertainty Bellman Equation and applying it via a separate pseudo-count network. These methods share a core design: the exploration bonus is \emph{additive} to the task reward and applies broadly across responses. SuS departs from this paradigm in two ways. First, it applies bonuses only to \emph{correct} completions, treating exploration as diversification of known-good strategies rather than discovery of unknown states. Second, the bonus operates under GRPO's group normalization, which (per Proposition 1) means any bonus applied to uniform batches produces phantom gradients. This constraint---unique to GRPO-style algorithms---makes the design space for exploration bonuses fundamentally different from the standard MDP setting where additive bonuses are standard practice and generally effective.

%=============================================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
%=============================================================================

\textbf{Single seed.} All results use seed 42 with bootstrap confidence intervals over problems (not seeds). The controlled ablation design (real PSE vs.\ random PSE vs.\ $\beta{=}0$, all sharing the same training pipeline) isolates the semantic contribution without requiring multi-seed validation, since all three variants experience the same per-seed noise. Cross-baseline comparisons (e.g., SuS vs.\ GRPO baseline) would benefit from multi-seed runs.

\textbf{Single model and scale.} We evaluate only Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct. The zero-variance preservation principle should generalize, but the optimal $\beta$ and the magnitude of improvement may differ at larger scales.

\textbf{Single dataset.} GSM8K is a well-studied benchmark approaching saturation for models of this class (base model: $\sim$68\% zero-shot). Evaluation on MATH-500 and AIME would test whether zero-variance preservation transfers to harder, more diverse problems. We expect the \emph{principle} to hold (it depends on GRPO's normalization, not the task), but the magnitude of SuS's improvement may differ.

\textbf{PSE not math-specific.} The all-MiniLM-L6-v2 encoder captures general semantic similarity, not mathematical reasoning structure. Despite this, the random-PSE ablation shows it provides measurable signal (+0.93pp over random vectors). A domain-specific encoder trained on mathematical reasoning chains could potentially amplify this effect.

\textbf{Hyperparameter selection.} The optimal $\beta{=}0.1$ was found by grid search on the same dataset. While the ablation shows robustness ($\beta \in [0.0, 0.2]$ all outperform Entropy Bonus), proper validation would use a held-out set.

\textbf{Small effective batch size.} With batch size 8 completions and $G{=}8$ per prompt, each optimizer step uses gradients from 2 prompts. This is substantially smaller than the 32–256 prompts per step commonly used in GRPO papers. Small batch sizes increase variance in advantage estimation; our conclusions about training stability may not transfer directly to larger-batch regimes.

\textbf{Missing ablation.} The difficulty tracker is not ablated against uniform $d{=}1$.

%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

This paper establishes two results. First, zero-variance preservation is a practical design principle for GRPO exploration rewards: Proposition 1 formalizes why violations produce phantom gradients, and all seven tested configurations are consistent with the principle---five preserving methods achieve Pass@1 $>$ 73\%, while two violating methods degrade. This principle complements existing approaches (DAPO's filtering, RL-ZVP's exploitation) by identifying when additive bonuses under group normalization become counterproductive.

Second, the random-PSE ablation demonstrates that the \emph{semantic content} of exploration signals matters: random unit vectors in an otherwise identical framework produce zero benefit (73.49\% $\approx$ 73.46\% no-bonus), while MiniLM embeddings yield +0.96pp. This controlled three-way comparison---real PSE, random PSE, no bonus---is the paper's strongest evidence, isolating semantic differentiation as the load-bearing component.

Both results generate concrete, falsifiable predictions. Any GRPO exploration method that preserves zero-variance batches should avoid the KL blowup and accuracy degradation observed in our violating configurations. A math-specific sentence encoder should amplify the gain over random PSE, while a domain-irrelevant encoder should reduce it. Scaling SuS to 7B+ models and harder benchmarks (MATH-500, AIME) would test whether the principle holds where the fraction of zero-variance batches differs substantially from the 65\% observed at 1.5B scale.
