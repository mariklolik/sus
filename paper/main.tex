\begin{abstract}
GRPO's group normalization zeroes out advantages for batches where all responses share the same reward---a built-in stability mechanism. Any per-sample exploration bonus with nonzero variance defeats this mechanism, producing \emph{phantom gradients} driven entirely by the bonus (Proposition~1). We call the resulting constraint \emph{zero-variance preservation} (ZVP). Across ten GRPO configurations on GSM8K with Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct, ZVP separates all six preserving configurations (Pass@1 ${>}$\,73\%) from all four violating ones ($-$1.75pp to $-$12.6pp). We instantiate the principle as Strategy-aware Surprise (SuS), which confines a semantic novelty bonus to correct responses in mixed-correctness batches. A three-way ablation---real sentence embeddings vs.\ random unit vectors vs.\ no bonus---shows that the gain requires per-sample semantic differentiation: random vectors yield zero benefit (+0.96pp, paired bootstrap $p{=}0.025$). Per-problem sign tests reveal a 3.1:1 advantage ratio on hard problems ($p < 10^{-10}$), and multi-seed partial training confirms the ZVP separation across three seeds. The principle is the main contribution; SuS is one instantiation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%=============================================================================

Group Relative Policy Optimization \citep[GRPO;][]{shao2024deepseekmath} normalizes rewards within each generation group (Eq.~\ref{eq:grpo_advantage}). This normalization, deployed at scale by DeepSeek-R1 \citep{guo2025deepseek} and SimpleRL \citep{zeng2025simplerl}, imposes a hard constraint on exploration bonus design: any per-sample bonus applied to batches where all responses share the same correctness label produces \emph{phantom gradients}---policy updates that reflect the bonus alone, not the task reward (Proposition~1). \emph{Where} bonuses are applied matters more than their magnitude.

The constraint is not theoretical. During GRPO training on GSM8K with Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct, ${\sim}65\%$ of late-training batches are \emph{zero-variance}: all $G$ responses are either all correct or all incorrect. Applying the \emph{same} novelty bonus to all batches (SuS-All) collapses Pass@1 to 67.3\% with $7{\times}$ KL divergence and completion lengths shrinking to 110 tokens; confining the identical bonus to mixed batches only yields 74.4\%---a 7.1pp gap from batch gating alone. A na\"ive entropy bonus collapses Pass@1 further to 61.4\%. Every method that confines bonuses to mixed-correctness batches maintains Pass@1 $\geq$ 73.5\%.

We call this constraint \emph{zero-variance preservation}: exploration bonuses must not inject per-sample variance into uniform batches.

To test what makes a good bonus \emph{within} preserving designs, we propose \textbf{Strategy-aware Surprise (SuS)}: a reward that adds a semantic novelty signal---cosine distance between frozen sentence embeddings---to correct responses in mixed batches only. SuS adds one frozen encoder (all-MiniLM-L6-v2, 22M parameters) and two scalars to standard GRPO.

The most controlled comparison is within the SuS pipeline: $\beta{=}0.1$ achieves 74.42\% Pass@1 vs.\ 73.46\% at $\beta{=}0.0$ (+0.96pp, paired bootstrap $p{=}0.025$), with the random-PSE ablation at 73.49\% confirming that the gain requires meaningful semantic content, not arbitrary perturbation. Against external baselines, SuS achieves the highest Pass@$k$ at all thresholds (CIs overlap with GRPO at 73.98\%\,$\pm$\,1.9pp, expected for a sub-1pp effect). Per-problem sign tests show a 3.1:1 advantage ratio on hard problems ($p < 10^{-10}$), with 25 previously unsolvable problems rescued.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Design principle.} We formalize zero-variance preservation (Proposition~1) and validate it across ten configurations: all six preserving methods exceed 73\% Pass@1; all four violating methods degrade ($\leq$72.2\%).
    \item \textbf{Instantiation.} SuS confines semantic novelty bonuses to correct responses in mixed batches, requiring only a frozen 22M-parameter encoder and two scalars.
    \item \textbf{Controlled ablation.} Real PSE vs.\ random PSE vs.\ no bonus: random vectors eliminate the entire +0.96pp gain, establishing that the bonus must carry semantic content.
    \item \textbf{Per-problem analysis.} Advantage ratios strengthen monotonically from 2.0:1 to 6.0:1 at harder thresholds ($p < 10^{-7}$), consistent with the mechanism: the bonus preserves minority correct strategies where collapse is worst.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{GRPO.}
Given a prompt $x$, GRPO generates $G$ completions $\{y_1, \ldots, y_G\}$ from the current policy $\pi_\theta$. Each completion receives a reward $r_i$. The advantage is computed by group normalization:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{A}_i = \frac{r_i - \mu_r}{\sigma_r + \epsilon}
    \label{eq:grpo_advantage}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_r$ and $\sigma_r$ are the mean and standard deviation of rewards within the group. The policy is updated via a clipped objective with a KL penalty:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = -\mathbb{E}\!\left[\min\!\left(\rho_i \hat{A}_i,\, \text{clip}(\rho_i, 1{\pm}\epsilon)\hat{A}_i\right)\right] + \lambda_{\text{KL}} D_{\text{KL}}
    \label{eq:grpo_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_i = \pi_\theta(y_i|x) / \pi_{\text{ref}}(y_i|x)$. Equations~\ref{eq:grpo_advantage}--\ref{eq:grpo_loss} are schematic; TRL's GRPOTrainer uses a per-token KL approximation.

\paragraph{Reward structure.}
Each completion receives a correctness reward $c_i \in \{0, 1\}$ (numerical answer match) and a format reward $f_i \in \{0, 1\}$ (\texttt{<answer>} tags present), weighted by 0.2.

\paragraph{Zero-variance batches.}
When all $G$ completions receive the same reward, $\sigma_r = 0$. TRL special-cases this: it sets all advantages to zero, producing no gradient for that prompt. As training progresses, easy problems become all-correct and hard problems become all-incorrect, so the zero-variance fraction rises. In our setup (Qwen2.5-1.5B on GSM8K, 2000 steps), ${\sim}65\%$ of late-training batches are zero-variance.

\paragraph{Notation.}
$c_i$: correctness; $f_i$: format; $n_c = \sum_i c_i$; $\mathcal{C} = \{i : c_i = 1\}$; $E(\cdot)$: frozen PSE; $\mathbf{e}_i = E(y_i)$; $\beta$: novelty coefficient; $d$: difficulty weight; $\alpha_d$: EMA decay; $\text{acc}_x$: per-problem accuracy estimate.

\section{Design Principle and SuS Instantiation}
\label{sec:method}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{The Zero-Variance Preservation Principle}
\label{sec:design_principles}

We state the central design principle and then describe two additional choices specific to SuS.

\textbf{P1: Zero-variance preservation (design principle).}
An exploration reward must not inject per-sample variance into uniform batches ($n_c = 0$ or $n_c = G$). SuS satisfies P1 by confining the novelty bonus to mixed batches.

\begin{proposition}[Phantom gradient]
\label{prop:phantom}
Let a uniform batch have base rewards $r_i = r$ for all $i$, and let an exploration bonus $\delta_i$ be added with $\operatorname{Var}(\delta) > 0$. Then GRPO's normalized advantage (Eq.~\ref{eq:grpo_advantage}) becomes $\hat{A}_i = (\delta_i - \bar{\delta}) / \sigma_\delta$, independent of the task reward $r$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$r_i' = r + \delta_i$, so $\mu_{r'} = r + \bar{\delta}$ and $\sigma_{r'} = \sigma_\delta$. Substituting: $\hat{A}_i = (r + \delta_i - r - \bar{\delta}) / \sigma_\delta = (\delta_i - \bar{\delta}) / \sigma_\delta$.
\end{proof}

The gradient from such a batch is a \emph{phantom gradient}: it updates the policy toward whichever completion received the highest bonus, irrespective of task performance.

\textbf{Why this matters.} TRL already handles $\sigma_r{=}0$ by zeroing all advantages---a built-in safety net that prevents gradient flow from uninformative batches. But any bonus with nonzero per-sample variance, even $\sigma{=}0.0004$ (SuS-All), makes $\sigma_r > 0$ and \emph{silently disables} this mechanism. The batch that would have produced zero gradient now produces a phantom gradient determined entirely by the bonus. With ${\sim}65\%$ of late-training batches uniform, phantom gradients can dominate. In Section~\ref{sec:zero_variance_analysis}, every method that triggers this shows elevated KL and degraded accuracy.

\textbf{P2: Correct-only novelty (SuS design choice).}
Bonuses differentiate only among correct responses. Adding noise to incorrect responses breaks P1 for all-incorrect batches and narrows the correctness reward gap.

\textbf{P3: Semantic measurement (SuS design choice).}
N-gram diversity conflates lexical and reasoning variation. Sentence embeddings capture a different signal. The random-PSE ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) confirms this choice: replacing MiniLM with random unit vectors eliminates the entire +0.96pp gain.

\subsection{Reward Structure}
\label{sec:reward_structure}

Given $G$ completions, SuS classifies the batch:

\textbf{Uniform batch} ($n_c = 0$ or $n_c = G$):
\begin{equation}
    r_i = c_i + 0.2 \cdot f_i
\end{equation}
No exploration bonus. Identical rewards for all completions.

\textbf{Mixed batch} ($0 < n_c < G$):
\begin{equation}
    r_i = \begin{cases}
        1.0 + 0.2 \cdot f_i + \beta \cdot \text{nov}_i \cdot d & \text{if } c_i = 1 \\
        0.0 + 0.2 \cdot f_i & \text{if } c_i = 0
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:sus_reward}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Post-hoc Strategy Encoder (PSE)}
\label{sec:pse}

A frozen sentence transformer (all-MiniLM-L6-v2; \citealp{reimers2019sentencebert, wang2020minilm}) maps completions to 384-d L2-normalized embeddings. Given correct completions $\{y_i : i \in \mathcal{C}\}$, intra-batch novelty is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{nov}_i = 1 - \frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}| - 1} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{C} \setminus \{i\}} \mathbf{e}_i^\top \mathbf{e}_j
    \label{eq:novelty}
\end{equation}
the average cosine distance from completion $i$ to all other correct completions. When $|\mathcal{C}| = 1$, $\text{nov}_i = 1$. The PSE is never fine-tuned.

\subsection{Difficulty Tracker}
\label{sec:difficulty}

An EMA of per-problem accuracy:
\begin{equation}
    \text{acc}_x \leftarrow \alpha_d \cdot \frac{n_c}{G} + (1 - \alpha_d) \cdot \text{acc}_x
\end{equation}
with $\alpha_d = 0.1$ and initial $\text{acc}_x = 0.5$. The difficulty weight $d = 1 - \text{acc}_x$ reduces the bonus for mastered problems, concentrating exploration on problems where diversity matters most.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Model and training.}
Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct \citep{yang2025qwen2} with LoRA \citep{hu2022lora} ($r{=}64$, $\alpha{=}128$), 2000 GRPO steps via TRL \citep{vonwerra2022trl}. $G{=}8$ completions per prompt, gradient accumulation 2, yielding 2 prompts per optimizer step---far smaller than the 32--256 typical of GRPO papers (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). Full hyperparameters: Table~\ref{tab:hyperparameters}.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
Full GSM8K test set \citep{cobbe2021training} (1319 problems), 16 samples per problem. Pass@$k$ via the unbiased estimator of \citet{chen2021evaluating}; 95\% CIs from 10{,}000 bootstrap resamples over problems.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
Four methods share the same base configuration:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{GRPO Baseline}: correctness + format rewards.
    \item \textbf{RS-GRPO} \citep{yu2025rsgrpo}: exponential reward shaping above the group median.
    \item \textbf{ProGRPO-ARM$^\dagger$} \citep{cui2025progrpo}: accuracy-modulated bonus. Our reimplementation updates the EMA per-completion within the group loop, introducing per-sample variance in uniform batches ($f_{\text{zv}} = 0.01$). This is likely an implementation divergence, not a property of the published method.
    \item \textbf{Entropy Bonus}: n-gram diversity ratio on all completions.
\end{itemize}

All results use seed 42 on a single NVIDIA H100 80GB GPU.

Within preserving designs, the semantic content of the signal separates methods that improve from those that do nothing (random PSE $\approx$ no bonus). \textbf{Semantic vs.\ random novelty.}
We replace MiniLM with random 384-d unit vectors ($\mathcal{N}(0, I)$, L2-normalized), keeping all else identical. In $\mathbb{R}^{384}$, random unit vectors have expected pairwise cosine similarity ${\approx}\,0$, so every correct response gets novelty ${\approx}\,1.0$---a near-uniform bonus of $\beta \cdot d$, ten times larger than the real PSE bonus but lacking per-sample variation.

Random PSE: 73.49\% [71.67, 75.29], indistinguishable from $\beta{=}0.0$ (73.46\%), 0.93pp below real PSE (74.42\%). Training dynamics match (Table~\ref{tab:ablation_pse}). A larger but uniform bonus gains nothing; a smaller but variable one yields +0.96pp. The gain requires \emph{per-sample semantic variation}, not merely a reward uplift for correct responses.

\subsection{Per-Problem Analysis}
\label{sec:perproblem}

The aggregate +0.44pp over baseline is modest. Per-problem analysis across 1319 test problems shows a localized effect.

\textbf{Hard problems.} At the baseline's bottom-quartile threshold ($\leq$8/16 correct, $N{=}333$), SuS wins 167 vs.\ 84 (2.0:1, sign test $p < 10^{-7}$). The ratio strengthens monotonically: at $\leq$4/16 ($N{=}221$), 3.1:1 ($p < 10^{-10}$); at $\leq$1/16 ($N{=}123$), 6.0:1 ($p < 10^{-9}$). For easy problems ($p{=}0.22$ across all 1319), there is no effect---exactly as predicted, since easy problems have no strategy collapse for the bonus to counter.

\textbf{Rescued problems.} SuS solves 25 problems where the baseline scores 0/16; 20 go the other way. These rescued problems drive the Pass@8 gain.

\textbf{Qualitative example.} On a multi-step word problem (driving + hiking distance), the baseline produces 0/8 correct: all eight assume 6 hours of driving and converge on the same wrong answer. SuS produces 3/8 correct via a minority strategy that correctly deduces driving time $= 6 - 1.5 = 4.5$ hours. The novelty bonus kept this minority alive.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Validation: MATH-500}
\label{sec:math500}

We evaluate the SuS and baseline checkpoints (trained on GSM8K) on MATH-500 \citep{hendrycks2021measuring}, a harder benchmark spanning algebra, geometry, number theory, and combinatorics (8 samples per problem).

Both methods perform comparably: SuS 39.57\% [36.05, 43.15] vs.\ baseline 39.90\% [36.35, 43.48]---a null result with fully overlapping CIs (difference $-0.33$pp, well within noise). This null separates two claims. The \emph{principle} transfers: both preserving checkpoints produce stable KL and normal completion lengths on a harder distribution, unlike violating methods that collapse during training. The \emph{encoder} does not: MiniLM's general-purpose embeddings carry no useful signal on competition mathematics, identifying encoder domain specificity as the bottleneck for stronger instantiations.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{ZVP as a design checklist.}
The principle is easy to state but fragile to implement. ProGRPO-ARM shows how: a per-completion EMA update (vs.\ per-batch) silently drops $f_{\text{zv}}$ from ${\sim}0.65$ to 0.01, costing 1.75pp. We recommend reporting $f_{\text{zv}}$ alongside KL and accuracy in GRPO exploration papers.

\paragraph{What the bonus must carry.}
The three-way comparison is the paper's most controlled result. Random vectors add a \emph{larger} bonus ($\text{nov} \approx 1.0$ vs.\ ${\sim}0.1$) but a near-uniform one, and gain nothing. This rules out two hypotheses: (1) that raising correct-response rewards suffices, and (2) that any per-sample perturbation helps. The bonus must vary \emph{meaningfully} across responses.

\paragraph{Where the bonus helps.}
The 3.1:1 hard-problem advantage ratio reflects a specific mechanism: the novelty bonus counteracts GRPO's tendency to suppress minority strategies on partially-learned problems. On mastered problems, ZVP ensures no interference. Stability on easy problems, diversity on hard ones---this is the practical consequence of the principle.

\paragraph{Beyond SuS.}
Proposition~1 applies to any GRPO variant that uses group normalization over a fixed response set. Alternatives that sidestep group normalization---DAPO's dynamic sampling (which discards uniform batches before computing advantages), token-level normalization, or RL-ZVP's separate advantage computation for uniform batches---avoid the phantom gradient mechanism entirely. For implementations that \emph{do} use standard group normalization (the majority, including TRL, OpenRLHF, and DeepSeek-R1's reported setup), ZVP marks the boundary: bonuses that are benign in standard RL can produce phantom gradients under group normalization.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
%=============================================================================

\paragraph{RL for LLM reasoning.}
GRPO \citep{shao2024deepseekmath} replaces the PPO critic with group-relative advantages. DeepSeek-R1 \citep{guo2025deepseek} scaled this to strong reasoning, SimpleRL \citep{zeng2025simplerl} showed simple setups are competitive, and Dr.GRPO \citep{liu2025drgrpo} corrected biases in the advantage estimator. DAPO \citep{yu2025dapo} proposed four techniques for stable 32B-scale GRPO, including Dynamic Sampling, which discards zero-variance batches.

\paragraph{Three responses to zero-variance batches.}
\textbf{Discard:} DAPO filters them out.
\textbf{Exploit:} RL-ZVP \citep{zhong2025noprompt} extracts signal from uniform batches via entropy-modulated rewards (+8.6pp at 7B). ReDit \citep{cheng2025redit} injects zero-mean noise. NGRPO \citep{wang2025ngrpo} hypothesizes a virtual maximum-reward sample. XRPO \citep{li2025xrpo} combines adaptive rollout allocation with advantage sharpening.
\textbf{Preserve:} SuS and RS-GRPO \citep{yu2025rsgrpo} leave uniform batches untouched ($f_{\text{zv}} \geq 0.63$ in our experiments).
The strategies are composable. RL-ZVP sidesteps phantom gradients by using a separate advantage computation for uniform batches, avoiding the mechanism we formalize.

\paragraph{Exploration in GRPO.}
\textbf{RS-GRPO} \citep{yu2025rsgrpo}: exponential reward shaping, preserves zero-variance.
\textbf{ProGRPO} \citep{cui2025progrpo}: accuracy-modulated bonuses gated on mixed batches.
\textbf{EDGE-GRPO} \citep{zhao2025edgegrpo}: entropy exploration with adaptive gating.
\textbf{i-MENTOR} \citep{wang2025imentor}: shows that group-normalized bonuses can invert advantage signs; SuS avoids this by targeting only correct responses ($1.0 \gg \beta \cdot \text{nov}_i \cdot d \leq 0.1$). Direct comparison requires matching i-MENTOR's 7B multi-dataset setup.
\textbf{CEEH} \citep{li2026ceeh} and \textbf{DRA-GRPO} \citep{zhang2025dragrpo}: difficulty-aware curricula and reward adjustment.
SuS combines zero-variance preservation, semantic novelty via sentence embeddings, and correct-only targeting.

\paragraph{Intrinsic motivation.}
Count-based methods \citep{bellemare2016unifying}, ICM \citep{pathak2017curiosity}, and RND \citep{burda2018exploration} add bonuses broadly. MERCI \citep{li2025merci} adapts count-based rewards to GRPO. SuS differs in two ways: bonuses target only correct completions (diversifying known-good strategies, not exploring unknown states), and the GRPO setting makes additive bonuses on uniform batches harmful (Proposition~1)---a constraint absent in standard MDPs.

\section{Scope and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
%=============================================================================

\textbf{Statistical design.} Full evaluations use seed 42 with bootstrap CIs over problems. The within-pipeline ablation shares per-seed noise, isolating the semantic contribution. Partial runs with seeds 123 and 456 (280--525 steps) confirm ZVP separation across seeds (Section~\ref{sec:zero_variance_analysis}); completing these runs is the most important extension.

\textbf{Scale.} Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct on GSM8K, cross-validated on MATH-500. The principle depends on GRPO's normalization, not the model or task. Open questions: does $f_{\text{zv}}$ change at 7B+ scale, and does a domain-specific encoder recover signal where MiniLM fails?

\textbf{Encoder.} MiniLM provides +0.93pp over random vectors on GSM8K but nothing on MATH-500. A math-specific encoder is the clearest path to a stronger instantiation.

\textbf{Batch size.} 2 prompts per optimizer step ($G{=}8$, accumulation 2)---far below the 32--256 standard. At larger batch sizes, more uniform prompts enter each gradient step, so phantom gradients from ZVP violations occupy a larger fraction. The principle should matter \emph{more} at scale; this is untested.

\textbf{Remaining ablations.} The difficulty tracker ($d = 1 - \text{acc}_x$) is not compared against uniform $d{=}1$; $\beta$ was grid-searched on training data.

%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

Zero-variance preservation---do not inject per-sample exploration variance into uniform-reward batches---follows from Proposition~1 and is validated across ten GRPO configurations: six preserving (Pass@1 $>$ 73\%), four violating ($\leq$72.2\%). The sharpest evidence is SuS-All: same bonus, all batches, 7.1pp collapse. Within preserving designs, the three-way PSE ablation shows the bonus must carry semantic content: random vectors yield nothing, real embeddings yield +0.96pp. Per-problem sign tests confirm the effect concentrates on hard problems (3.1:1, $p < 10^{-10}$), where minority strategies are most at risk.

The principle makes a falsifiable prediction: \emph{any GRPO exploration bonus that violates ZVP will show elevated KL and degraded accuracy compared to a preserving variant with the same bonus design}. As GRPO becomes the default for LLM reasoning, the interaction between bonus structure and group normalization warrants the same attention as the bonus itself.
